Preventive Care Checklist

Regular preventive care helps catch health issues early when they're easier to treat. The American Animal Hospital Association (AAHA) recommends wellness exams at least once a year for adult dogs, and more frequently for puppies and seniors.

Weekly Care

  • Check ears for redness, odor, or discharge
  • Brush teeth (daily is ideal, but weekly at minimum)
  • Inspect coat and skin for lumps, bumps, or irritation
  • Monitor food and water intake
  • Observe energy levels and bathroom habits

Monthly Care

  • Administer parasite preventatives (as prescribed by your vet)
  • Check nails and trim if needed (or as often as growth requires)
  • Weigh your dog and track any changes
  • Clean bedding and toys
  • Assess body condition (can you feel ribs? is there a waist?)

Annual Veterinary Visits Should Include

  • Complete physical examination
  • Core vaccinations (as recommended by your vet based on risk)
  • Heartworm test
  • Fecal exam for intestinal parasites
  • Dental assessment
  • Discussion of diet, behavior, and any concerns
  • Blood work (especially important for seniors)
  • Weight and body condition evaluation

Why Preventive Care Matters

Dogs age faster than humans, and many health conditions develop silently. Annual exams help detect problems like dental disease, kidney issues, or early-stage arthritis before they become serious. According to veterinary organizations, preventive care is more effective and often less costly than treating advanced illness.

Nutrition Basics

Choosing Quality Dog Food

Look for foods that meet AAFCO (Association of American Feed Control Officials) standards. The label should state the food is "complete and balanced" for your dog's life stage. This means the food has been formulated or tested to meet established nutritional requirements.

The World Small Animal Veterinary Association (WSAVA) recommends selecting foods from companies that:

  • Employ full-time qualified nutritionists
  • Own their manufacturing facilities
  • Conduct and publish research
  • Provide detailed product information upon request

Puppies (Under 1-2 Years)

Require food labeled for "growth" or "all life stages." Need more calories, protein, and specific nutrients like calcium for developing bones. Large breeds need controlled growth formulas.

Adults (1-7 Years)

Need food labeled for "adult maintenance." Focus on maintaining healthy weight. Adjust portions based on activity level and body condition.

Seniors (7+ Years)

May benefit from senior formulas with adjusted calories and joint-supporting nutrients. Discuss specific needs with your vet, as senior dogs vary widely.

A Note on Trendy Diets

Grain-free, raw, and homemade diets have become popular, but they require caution:

  • Grain-free diets: The FDA has investigated a potential link between certain grain-free foods and heart disease (dilated cardiomyopathy) in dogs. Discuss with your vet before choosing grain-free.
  • Raw diets: Carry risks of bacterial contamination for both pets and humans. Not recommended by AVMA or AAHA without veterinary guidance.
  • Homemade diets: Difficult to balance properly. If you prefer homemade, work with a board-certified veterinary nutritionist.

The safest approach is to choose commercial foods that meet AAFCO standards and discuss any dietary changes with your veterinarian.

Parasite & Seasonal Safety

Year-Round Prevention Is Key

Many pet owners think parasites are only a summer problem, but veterinary organizations recommend year-round prevention. Parasites can survive indoors during winter, and climate variations mean risks exist in every season. Consistent prevention is more effective than trying to catch up after an infestation.

Fleas & Ticks

Beyond itching, fleas can cause allergic dermatitis and transmit tapeworms. Ticks carry serious diseases like Lyme disease, ehrlichiosis, and Rocky Mountain spotted fever. Use vet-recommended preventatives consistently.

Heartworm

Transmitted by mosquitoes, heartworm disease is serious and potentially fatal. Prevention is simple and effective; treatment is expensive, lengthy, and hard on your dog. Monthly preventatives are standard.

Intestinal Parasites

Roundworms, hookworms, whipworms, and tapeworms can affect dogs of any age. Regular fecal testing and deworming (as recommended by your vet) help keep these in check.

Zoonotic Awareness: Protecting Your Family

Some parasites can spread from pets to humans, especially children, elderly individuals, and those with weakened immune systems. Examples include roundworms, hookworms, and certain tick-borne diseases. Good hygiene practices help reduce risk:

  • Wash hands after handling pets, especially before eating
  • Pick up dog waste promptly from yards and public areas
  • Keep children's sandboxes covered
  • Maintain your pet's parasite prevention schedule
  • Schedule regular veterinary fecal exams

Seasonal Hazards

Summer Safety

  • Never leave dogs in parked carsâ€”temperatures rise dangerously fast
  • Provide shade and fresh water outdoors
  • Walk during cooler morning or evening hours
  • Test pavement with your handâ€”if too hot for you, it's too hot for paws
  • Watch for signs of heatstroke: excessive panting, drooling, lethargy

Winter Safety

  • Limit time outdoors in extreme cold, especially for small or short-coated dogs
  • Wipe paws after walks to remove salt and chemicals
  • Check for antifreeze spillsâ€”it's toxic and tastes sweet to dogs
  • Consider a coat or sweater for dogs with thin fur
  • Keep dogs away from frozen ponds and lakes
